Case 02 · Patient: Kofi M., age 24 (fictional) · Week 2

Last week you measured how much water Kofi lost and how his body fought to defend blood pressure. Now go to the membrane. The clear "rice-water" stool is not random: a toxin has hijacked a chloride channel in his gut lining, and water is following salt out into the lumen. The cure, oral rehydration therapy, is one of the most elegant applications of membrane transport in all of medicine. This week you work out both the disease and the fix from the transporters up.

Given

  • Osmolarity (mOsm/L) = molar concentration × number of dissociated particles. NaCl → 2 particles (Na⁺ + Cl⁻). Glucose → 1 particle.
  • Plasma osmolarity ≈ 290 mOsm/L.
  • Hypotonic < 290, isotonic ≈ 290, hypertonic > 290 (relative to plasma).

Part 2 of 4 · Interpret

The mechanism: how cholera empties the gut

Cholera toxin locks an enzyme on, flooding the gut-lining cell (enterocyte) with the signaling molecule cAMP. High cAMP holds the apical CFTR chloride channel open. The result is a cascade across the membrane that pulls water into the lumen.

Transporters in play (enterocyte)

  • Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase (basolateral): pumps 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in, using ATP.
  • NKCC1 cotransporter (basolateral): brings Na⁺, K⁺, and 2 Cl⁻ into the cell down the Na⁺ gradient.
  • CFTR channel (apical): Cl⁻ exits into the lumen when open.

Part 3 of 4 · Reason & Calculate

The fix: why oral rehydration works

Cholera toxin does not touch the SGLT1 transporter on the apical membrane. SGLT1 carries Na⁺ and glucose together into the cell, powered by the inward Na⁺ gradient. Where Na⁺ goes, water follows. This is why a drink of salt and sugar can absorb water even while the gut is pouring it out.

WHO reduced-osmolarity oral rehydration solution (per liter)

  • Sodium (Na⁺) 75 mM, chloride (Cl⁻) 65 mM, glucose 75 mM, potassium (K⁺) 20 mM, citrate (count as 10 mOsm/L).
  • Total target osmolarity for this formula: about 245 mOsm/L.
