Case 02 · Patient: Kofi M., age 24 (fictional) · Week 3

Kofi was rehydrated, but the diarrhea did not only cost him water. Stool is rich in potassium and bicarbonate, and after two days his plasma potassium has fallen to 2.5 mM (normal 3.5 to 5.0). He feels weak, and his monitor shows an irregular heartbeat. Potassium is the ion that sets the resting membrane potential, so a change this size is electrical, not just chemical. This week you calculate what his cells "feel" and predict the danger.

Part 1 of 4 · Calculate

Equilibrium potentials with the Nernst equation

The Nernst equation gives the membrane voltage at which a single ion would be at equilibrium (no net movement). At body temperature, for a monovalent ion:

Eion = (61 / z) × log10 ( [ion]outside / [ion]inside )  (mV); z = charge (use +1 for Na⁺ and K⁺)

Part 2 of 4 · Reason

Why the resting potential sits near EK

A real cell is permeable to several ions at once, but at rest it is far more permeable to K⁺ than to Na⁺ (many K⁺ leak channels are open). The resting membrane potential (RMP) therefore sits close to EK but is pulled slightly positive by the small Na⁺ leak.

Q2. A neuron's measured RMP is −70 mV. Your EK from Part 1 is more negative than that. Explain why the real RMP is a little less negative than EK, naming the ion responsible.
Q3. Predict, in words, what would happen to the RMP if you doubled the membrane's Na⁺ permeability while holding everything else constant. Toward which equilibrium potential would RMP move?
